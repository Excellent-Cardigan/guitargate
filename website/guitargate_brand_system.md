# Guitargate Brand System

*Copy & content deliverable — Version 0.5, May 2026*

> Guitargate is the umbrella. Teleport and Voxbox are the tools. The community is the whole point.

This document contains written copy for every section of the Guitargate brand style guide, structured to mirror the Mau completed-example layout page by page. Hand this to your designer alongside the Mau visual reference as the source of all written content.

### What changed in v0.5

Version 0.5 is a tactical follow-up to v0.4 that locks in the last open sub-brand: **Voxbox**. The brand strategy, archetype, palette structure, and verbal identity established in v0.4 all carry forward unchanged.

The four confirmed decisions driving v0.5:

1. **Voxbox wordmark:** confirmed as **VO(X)BOX** — central X enclosed in parentheses, set in Voxbox Orange `#FF7420`. The earlier working **VO⋈BOX** glyph is retired across the brand book.
2. **Voxbox device function:** confirmed as a two-channel vocal preamp pedal designed primarily to feed Teleport. Sing into Voxbox (XLR mic in), route the signal into Teleport (1/4" out), and vocals enter the Guitargate system the same way Teleport's guitar signal does — looped, uploaded to the app, routed to other members. Dual-vocalist standalone use is supported via the two channels but is secondary to the Voxbox→Teleport workflow.
3. **Voxbox tagline:** *Vocals, made less lonely.* — parallel construction to the brand's existing voice line *Guitar, made less lonely.*
4. **Voxbox Channel Two green:** a secondary product-UX color (LED, channel label, signal-flow accents on hardware) introduced as **scoped to the pedal interface only** — not a Voxbox-brand accent.

### What changed in v0.4

Version 0.4 applies the **Everyman (primary) + Caregiver (secondary)** archetype to the brand system. All visual systems (palette HEX values, WCAG audit, logo construction math, grid equation, typography specs, device naming rule) are archetype-neutral and carry forward from v0.3 unchanged. Verbal identity, brand strategy sections, voice-in-use, photography direction, and a handful of headlines have been refined.

The five confirmed decisions driving this version:

1. **Trueline:** *Keep it in your hands.* — the brand mantra, replacing the previous Sage-coded line as the line of record in the brand book. The previous "Ultimate Guitar Learning Experience" line is retired from the brand book and remains a legacy marketing headline elsewhere as needed.
2. **Brand Value — Craft:** reframed from Creator-flavored ("we care about how things are made") to Caregiver-flavored ("everything we put in your hands gets the same care we'd give a guitar we built ourselves").
3. **Experience Principle #5:** *Ship the Gear* replaced with *Find Your Band* — restating the principle at the outcome level (what members get) rather than the output level (what we make).
4. **Our Story:** rewritten using Michael's own words verbatim from his founder email, including the *Stargate-not-Watergate* and *little-voice-microphone* framings.
5. **Photography:** Warm & Lived-In becomes the dominant 80% of the visual system. The abstract direction is preserved at ~20% and reframed from cosmic/Martian terrain toward *intimate textures* — close-up textures of practice spaces, wood grain, amp tolex, fingertip calluses, wear marks on a pickguard. Same compositional discipline, archetype-aligned subject matter.

## 1.4 Brand Architecture (Page 7b)

*This page does not exist in Mau. It's a required addition for Guitargate because the system includes a parent brand plus named devices. It establishes the relationship that every downstream section depends on.*

### Intro paragraph

Guitargate is an umbrella brand that contains a growing family of devices and gear. Understanding which brand is which — and when to use each — is the foundation of the whole system.

### The three layers

**1. Guitargate — the parent brand**

The community, the school, the place where players meet. Guitargate is the central identity that every product, every lesson, and every member belongs to. The Guitargate mark always appears on anything we make.

**2. Devices & Gear — the tools**

Physical and digital products that members use to create, share, and collaborate. Each device has its own name (Teleport, Voxbox, future gear), its own accent color, and its own wordmark treatment, but always lives inside the Guitargate system — it shares the same typography, the same foundational palette, and always appears with or near the Guitargate mark.

**3. The Community — the whole point**

Everything we make exists to serve the community. The parent brand is the meeting place. The tools are what members use once they get there. Neither works without the other.

### The BOSS model

For designers new to the system: think BOSS pedals. Every BOSS pedal has its own name (DD-3, DS-1, RV-6), its own pedal color, and its own personality — but every single one carries the BOSS wordmark on top, uses the same typography, and is clearly part of one family. That's how Guitargate, Teleport, and Voxbox relate.

## 2.4 Writing Guidelines (Page 11b)

### Do

| Guideline | Example |
|---|---|
| Write in first person plural — use "we", not "Guitargate" | ✅ "We built Teleport so you never have to practice alone." |
| Lead with insight, not feature lists | ✅ "Teleport is how practice becomes community." |
| Use contractions | ✅ "You don't have to figure this out alone." |
| Write short sentences when the point is simple | ✅ "Practice is better with bandmates." |
| Name the 'why' before the 'what' | ✅ "The reason this chord progression works is the tension of the IV before the I resolves it." |
| Call players 'players' or 'members', never 'users' or 'students' | ✅ "Our members are 200,000 strong." |
| Acknowledge the difficulty before promising the upside | ✅ "Practicing alone is hard. That's the whole problem. So we built a place where you don't have to." |

### Don't

| Guideline | Example |
|---|---|
| Don't use AI tell-words | ❌ "Our stunning new pedal revolutionizes the way you play." |
| Don't write about Guitargate from the outside | ❌ "Guitargate is an online guitar education platform." |
| Don't use passive voice | ❌ "Lessons are taught by world-class musicians." |
| Don't use industry jargon | ❌ "Onboard via our learner activation funnel." |
| Don't over-promise transformation | ❌ "Become a virtuoso in 30 days." Promise belonging instead: "You'll have people in your corner." |

## 2.5 Vocabulary (Page 11c)

### Use this

players · members · bandmates · the community · the room · in your corner · show up · stay · plug in · tune up · jam · session · lesson · pro · membership · loop · take · REACTS · Teleport · Voxbox

### Avoid this

users · students of the platform · learners · customers · subscribers · audience · content · courseware · end users · class (use lesson) · stunning · revolutionary · game-changing · industry-leading

> 🟧 **NOTE** — *Students* is a delicate case. Michael does teach, and "student" isn't wrong in casual conversation. In brand voice, however, the word of record is *member* or *player* — because those name a relationship (you belong here) rather than a role (you're learning from us). When writing about lessons, write about *taking a lesson* without using "student" as a noun.

## 3.3 Device Logos (Page 14b)

Each device wordmark follows the Device Naming Rule (see §3.8 below).

### Teleport

**TELEP(O)RT** — central O replaced by a portal glyph in Teleport Violet `#8144FF`. Remaining letters in near-black `#242321`.

### Voxbox

**VO(X)BOX** — central X enclosed in parentheses, set in Voxbox Orange `#FF7420`. Remaining letters in near-black `#242321`.

Voxbox is the vocalist's way into the Guitargate system. It's a two-channel vocal preamp pedal — XLR microphone in and 1/4" out per channel, with independent gain knobs and foot switches — designed primarily to feed Teleport. Sing into Voxbox; route the signal out into Teleport; the voice now does everything a Teleport guitar signal does — loops, uploads to the app, travels between members. The two channels let two vocalists run together when they want to (or one vocalist with a secondary mic source), but that's the secondary use case. The main idea is simpler: Voxbox is how vocals enter the same network the guitarists are already on.

## 3.7 Logo Misuse (Page 17)

**Do Not:**

1. Squash, stretch or distort
2. Apply effects (drop shadow, bevel, glow)
3. Recolor outside the approved system palette
4. Rotate
5. Change the typeface
6. Use weak contrast
7. Resize individual elements
8. Place on complex or busy backgrounds
9. Add a stroke
10. Pair a device wordmark with a different device's accent color
11. Use a device wordmark without the parent mark in co-branded contexts

## 3.8 Device Naming Rule (Page 17b)

> *One letter, one icon, one color.*

The naming rule exists so every device in the Guitargate system reads as part of one family at a glance — members shouldn't have to read the wordmark to know which world they're in.

**One letter** — exactly one letter from the device name, never two, never zero. The letter chosen should be central to the word visually and phonetically — the letter that "holds" the name.

**One icon** — the replacement glyph must literally or figuratively describe what the device does. Teleport's (O) is a portal that sends sound between rooms. Voxbox's (X) is parentheses framing the channel — an enclosure for the voice it carries. A future compressor might customize the central letter with a VU-meter glyph; a future looper might use a cyclical/arrow-ring.

**One color** — the customized letter is rendered in the device's sub-brand accent color (see §4.3). The rest of the wordmark is rendered in the system's near-black. No device uses more than one accent letter.

### Examples currently in the system

- **TELEP(O)RT** — portal (O) in Teleport Violet `#8144FF`
- **VO(X)BOX** — parenthesized (X) in Voxbox Orange `#FF7420`

### For future devices

> 🟥 **NEEDS INPUT** — When naming a new device, run it through this checklist: (1) Is the name a single word or hyphenate at most? (2) Does it have a clear center letter that can be customized? (3) Is there a glyph or icon that describes what the device does? (4) Does the new accent color pair well with the existing sub-brand palette — i.e., is it distinguishable from Guitargate Blue, Teleport Violet, and Voxbox Orange at a glance?

*Page 18 section divider. Sub-sections: 4.1 The Foundation · 4.2 The 60/30/10 Rule · 4.3 Guitargate Palette · 4.4 Teleport Palette · 4.5 Voxbox Palette · 4.6 Neutral Scale · 4.7 Dark Mode Usage*

## 4.1 The Foundation (Page 18b)

The Guitargate color system is built around one shared foundation and a growing library of sub-brand accent palettes. Every brand in the system — parent and device — shares the same near-black and cream foundation. Each brand adds a single accent color, expressed as a three-swatch ramp: dark tint, pure accent, light tint.

- Guitargate → Blue ramp
- Teleport → Violet ramp
- Voxbox → Orange ramp (pending sign-off)

## 4.2 The 60/30/10 Color Rule

Every frame, image, post, and poster must conform to this ratio:

> **60% — Dominant neutral.** Warm near-black, cream, pale sand, or geological mid-tone. Never a brand color.

> **30% — Secondary tone.** The dark zone — rock mass, shadow, void.

> **10% — Accent.** One named brand color per frame. White is neutral, not an accent.

**Device packaging exception:** Sub-brand color is the 60%, near-black is the 30%, cream/white type is the 10%.

# Section 7.0 — Brand Photography

*Page 35 section divider. Two photographic directions, with the dominant share (~80%) given to the Warm & Lived-In direction and the remaining ~20% to the Intimate Texture direction.*

## 7.1 Photographic Style — Overview

Guitargate's photography sits in two registers. The dominant register is **Warm & Lived-In** — the everyday rooms where music actually happens. The secondary register is **Intimate Texture** — close-up abstract compositions used for poster moments, campaign hero pieces, and album-art-style packaging accents.

The two registers share a foundation: warm and natural tones (creams, woods, browns, muted grays), real materials, real light, real wear.

## 7.2 Warm & Lived-In (Dominant — ~80% of usage)

This is the photographic home of the brand. Real practice spaces. Hands on instruments. Members in their actual rooms. Members on stage at open mics. Teleport on a worn rug next to a half-finished cup of coffee. Voxbox on a stand in a real venue. Wood grain, amp tolex, scuffed pedalboards, taped setlists, fingertip calluses.

### Subject rules

- Player, instrument, and device as primary focal points. Never product-on-seamless.
- Real members and real teachers, never stock photography.
- Spaces should look used, not staged. The bed is unmade. The plant needs water. The dog might be in the frame.

### Light rules

- Natural light where possible. Window light, lamp light, evening light.
- Soft depth of field — the room should feel like the player's room, not a studio.
- Warm color temperature. Cool clinical light is a Misuse.

### Mood

Lived-in, warm, vulnerable, present. The photograph should feel like you walked into someone's practice room mid-session.

## 7.3 Intimate Texture (Secondary — ~20% of usage)

The abstract photographic direction, used for poster moments, campaign hero pieces, and packaging accents where a more graphic, designed image is needed.

### Subject

Close-up textures from the world Guitargate's members already live in:
- Wood grain (guitar bodies, practice room floors, vintage amplifier cabinets)
- Amp tolex, grille cloth, and weave patterns
- Fingertip calluses, knuckle wrinkles, hand wear
- Pickguard scuffs, fretboard wear, headstock chips
- Setlist tape on a stage floor, gaff tape on a cable, sharpie marks on a strap
- Cable braid, knob detail, pedalboard texture

### Composition

The compositional discipline from the original abstract direction carries forward: diagonal split, full striation, concentric ring, or color field. The 60/30/10 rule applies. The annotation layer (crosshairs, bounding boxes, CoFo Sans coordinate text) sits above the image and below the type. Same architectural approach — vivid focal-point color zone bounded by textural neutral framing — but the source material is intimate, not cosmic.

### What changed in v0.4

The previous direction sourced abstract imagery from aerial geological landscapes (Martian terrain, desert sediment). v0.4 keeps the compositional grammar but shifts the source material from *vast* to *intimate*. This preserves all existing design infrastructure (annotation layers, four image types, 60/30/10) while bringing the photographic content in line with Everyman + Caregiver archetype.

> 🟧 **NOTE** — Existing geological MJ outputs from the v0.3 era do not need to be discarded immediately. They can continue to be used for specific applications (album-cover-style poster moments) but should be supplemented and gradually replaced with intimate-texture sources as the new library is built.

## 7.4 Annotation Rules

| Image type | Max crosshairs | Max dot points | Max text blocks | Max bounding boxes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Diagonal split | 3 | 3 | 1 | 1 |
| Full striation | 2 | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| Concentric ring | 1 | 4 | 1 | 1 |
| Color field | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 |

**Warm side / dark side rule:** Type and coordinate text on the warm/pale side. Brand mark on the dark side. Never cross the boundary between the two zones.

## 7.5 Photography Direction

Neutral and natural tones — creams, warm woods, browns, muted grays. Device accent colors (Teleport Violet, Voxbox accent) act as accents against neutral backgrounds. Subject centered, soft depth of field.

## 7.6 Composition

Subject centered in frame, soft depth of field. For device-focused photography, frame the pedal so the wordmark (and accent color) is clearly legible. Include a wider view of the surrounding environment and close-up shots of the hardware. The room around the subject is part of the photograph, never cropped out for a "cleaner" shot.

## 7.7 Image Library

Library should include: members playing at home, device hardware in use, live community moments, brand in real-world contexts, and a parallel stream of intimate texture sources for the secondary photographic register.
